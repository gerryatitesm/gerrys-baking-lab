## Ingredients

| Ingredient                        | Quantity |
|-----------------------------------|----------|
| All-purpose flour                 | 2¼ cups  |
| Brown sugar                       | 1¼ cups  |
| White sugar                       | ¼ cup    |
| Unsalted butter, softened         | 1 cup    |
| Large eggs                        | 2        |
| Vanilla extract                   | 2 tsp    |
| Baking soda                       | 1 tsp    |
| Salt                              | 1 tsp    |
| Semi-sweet chocolate chips        | 2 cups   |

> **Control batch:** Used 1½ cups white sugar and no brown sugar while keeping all other ingredients constant.

## Procedure

1. Preheated oven to 350°F (177°C) and prepared baking sheets with parchment paper.
2. Mixed butter, brown sugar, and white sugar until creamy and smooth.
3. Added eggs and vanilla extract, mixing thoroughly after each addition.
4. Combined flour, baking soda, and salt in a separate bowl.
5. Slowly incorporated dry ingredients into the wet mixture until a dough formed.
6. Folded chocolate chips evenly throughout the dough.
7. Scooped dough into equal portions and placed them 2 inches apart on baking sheets.
8. Baked for approximately 11 minutes until edges were lightly browned.
9. Allowed cookies to cool for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack.
10. Repeated the process for the control batch.

## Observed Results

- **Experimental Batch:** Cookies spread to approximately 4.5 cm in diameter. They developed a deeper golden-brown color and emitted a strong caramel-like aroma. The centers remained soft and chewy even after cooling.
- **Control Batch:** Cookies remained slightly smaller, averaging 4.0 cm in diameter. They were lighter in color and noticeably crisper throughout.
- After two hours, the experimental cookies still felt moist and flexible, whereas the control cookies had become firmer and more brittle.

## Conclusions

The hypothesis was supported. Increasing the proportion of brown sugar resulted in cookies with a softer texture, greater moisture retention, and enhanced flavor complexity.

Possible explanations include:

1. **Moisture Retention:** Brown sugar contains molasses, which helps retain water and slows drying after baking.
2. **Enhanced Browning:** Molasses contributes compounds that encourage browning reactions, producing richer color and flavor.
3. **Texture Modification:** The additional moisture created a more tender crumb and prolonged chewiness.

**Future Investigation:** Examine whether refrigerating the dough for 24 hours before baking further improves flavor development and texture.
